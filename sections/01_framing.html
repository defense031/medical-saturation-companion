1 · Framing

A great-power war could deliver wounded faster than a forward surgical team can operate. Whether it does, and by how much, is what we test. We are left to determine which casualties died of preventable wounds, the ones faster or better-arranged care would have saved, and which of non-preventable ones. Force design can reduce only the preventable deaths, which a capacity count misses. This study estimates how many there are, and which interventions prevent the most: faster evacuation, more surgery, prolonged field care, or how the teams are positioned.

Three findings drive the resourcing decision:

  • Size for the surge. A team that keeps pace with the daily average can be overwhelmed by one large arrival of casualties at once. Averaging over an entire day can mask the risk from those arrival surges.
  • Speed beats capacity. Time from wounding to the table matters more for survival than the number of tables. In our model the first hour of delay costs about 16 mortality points, more than a second table would save.
  • Evacuation has two halves with opposite fixes. Moving the wounded to surgery is threat-limited: contested air keeps the helicopters on the ground. Moving them out of theater is lift-limited: the rear hospitals have capacity to spare, but long-range aircraft are few. A fix for one does nothing for the other.

2 · The decision this serves

The question that settles the resourcing call
At what casualty arrival rate does a forward surgical team flip from wound-limited death, set by physiology, to capacity-limited death, set by waiting? And which lever moves that flip point the most?

The flip separates the deaths into two groups. Below it, wound biology sets the toll, and more resources save almost no one. Above it, the team's shortfall adds preventable deaths, the share that force structure, positioning, evacuation policy, and triage can reduce. Summed over a campaign, it is combat power lost for nothing: Q187's "strategic impact," Q522's "structural adjustment." Today's teams are sized near the peer planning casualty rate, while high-intensity offensive operations run several times higher, so the flip is already in range within the engagement pulse, even where the daily average still has margin.

3 · The conditions that decide the answer

The casualty-rate regime is the condition that turns surgical capacity from surplus to binding. At the peer planning rate the team has margin; at high-intensity offensive rates the engaged-echelon pulse overruns it. The analysis sweeps the rate rather than betting on one scenario. Contested forward air is the second condition: it lengthens the time the wounded spend reaching surgery, which is where most of the preventable death comes from.

"Air" means two different aircraft, and the distinction runs through the whole analysis.

  • Forward, getting the wounded to surgery (point of injury → team): threat-limited. The lift is rotary-wing casualty evacuation; contested air over the close fight grounds or downs it, and the wounded die in transit. These fixes need no contested route: prolonged field care, blood pushed forward, dispersion.
  • Rear, getting the wounded out (team → definitive care): capacity-limited. The lift is fixed-wing strategic transport flying from rear airfields; the rear hospitals flex, and a finite airlift fleet is what binds. The fix is procurement: airframes and the en-route care crews who ride them.

So "air superiority" in this study is the narrow thing the forward axis needs, a permissive environment for rotary casualty evacuation over the close fight. These are the two halves of Q187's "contested domains or burgeoning logistical requirements," and they take opposite fixes.

4 · The levers

Each lever acts on one piece of the problem. The decisive question for force design is whether a lever moves the flip point itself or only the death level above it.
