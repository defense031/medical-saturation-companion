The research question

The research question
In a great-power war, does the casualty rate outrun the Army's forward surgical capacity, and at what point does it break?
Made precise: find the casualty arrival rate $\lambda^{*}$ at which forward surgical capacity saturates, and the rate at which the wounded die-of-wounds once it does, both as a function of how long contested transport holds casualties before evacuation.

The answer is three numbers:

  • The threshold $\lambda^{*}$ at which operating-table-hours saturate (utilization $\rho\to 1$) and the died-of-wounds fraction transitions sharply.
  • The curve $\text{DOW}(\lambda,T)$ above that threshold: how many more die per increment of overload.
  • The evacuation-policy sensitivity $\partial\,\text{DOW}/\partial T$: added deaths per added holding-day.

Scope is the forward surgical node in a single theater, concretely the Forward Resuscitative Surgical Detachment (FRSD), the surgical team that augments a Role 2 Enhanced medical company, whose published signature (2 tables, 135 min/case, ~10 cases/24 h) sets the capacity throughout. The roles-of-care tandem (FRSD → post-surgical holding → onward) enters as an extension. Downstream, strategic aeromedical-evacuation lift out of theater binds before beds do, because definitive-care beds flex and offload to civilian systems while the airlift fleet does not. Sizing that fleet is an adjacent problem we leave to other work.

Pre-registered hypotheses

Ordered so the lead claim is checkable against data that exists.

H1: saturation is a within-pulse phenomenon (the checkable lead). On daily-average load a single doctrinal node keeps margin even at high-intensity offensive rates; it saturates inside the engagement pulse, the great-power regime Q187 names, where casualties arrive far faster than tables turn over: $$\lambda_{\text{peak}} \;\gg\; s\mu \quad\text{inside the battle pulse.}$$ The calibration audit ran this check. The offensive WIA rate runs roughly 3–4× the lower-intensity rate (9.74–15.6 vs 3.54/1,000/day), but at a defensible surgical fraction the daily-average load stays under capacity: $\rho\approx 0.2$ at the Korea rate and $\rho\approx 0.4$–$0.8$ at WWII-offensive rates ($f_{\text{surg}}=0.10$–$0.12$, $s\mu=10$/day). The burst is what saturates the node while the daily-average load still has room: a measured pulse (Soroka 83 casualties/hr) exceeds node throughput (~0.4 surgical cases/hr) by one-to-two orders of magnitude, sharpened by $\mu$-degradation under a contested forward fight (see Results). Falsified if the node absorbs the engaged-echelon pulse without died-of-wounds rising.
H2: the cliff. Past the threshold, died-of-wounds rises sharply, an elbow in the curve: $$\text{DOW}(\lambda)\;\approx\;\left(1-\frac{s\mu}{\lambda}\right)^{\!+}.$$ Computed (Results): the threshold location holds at $\lambda^{*}=s\mu$ and the fluid law is the above-threshold asymptote, but the survival clock softens the kink, since abandonment is nonzero below $\rho=1$ (casualties renege while waiting). The elbow holds its location and bends more gently than the idealized law. Falsified if no transition appears; one does.
H3: the binding constraint. At the threshold, what binds is operating-table-hours. Falsified if beds saturate first under doctrinal ratios.
H4: the evacuation-timeline lever. Lengthening the time $T$ from wounding to surgery raises DOW at fixed $\lambda$: $$\frac{\partial\,\text{DOW}}{\partial T} \;>\; 0.$$ Computed and validated (Results): $\partial\,\text{DOW}/\partial T$ is steep and positive, ~16 mortality points per added hour in the first hour, flattening as it saturates, so the golden hour falls out of the model. The Gaza (fast) / Ukraine (slow) bracket reproduces the direction. Disentanglement: the pre-registered $T$ is the forward timeline (time-to-surgery), which the bracket tests; this hypothesis as first written also gestured at the doctrinal rear "days held" (ATP 4-02.55 Table D-1's rising "died-in-hospital" factor), but that rear effect is the emergent back-pressure of Results Finding 4, reported as structure.

The two to defend hardest in review: H1's checkable inequality and H3's binding-constraint flip.

The go/no-go test

Everything downstream depends on one inequality, computable from data in hand:
$$\lambda_{\text{obs}} \;\overset{?}{>}\; s\mu.$$
Compute $s\mu$ for a doctrinal node from ATP 4-02.25, compare to echelon casualty rates against published surgical throughput. The inequality clears, and the full estimand follows from the model: $\lambda^{*}=s\mu$, the DOW elbow, and the evacuation-timeline slope $\partial\,\text{DOW}/\partial T$ are computed in Results.

Constraints, limitations, assumptions

Assumptions behind the sharp tipping point

If X, Y, Z hold, then the node has a saturation tipping point at $\lambda^{*}=s\mu$: below it DOW is wound-limited and nearly flat in $\lambda$; above it DOW rises as $\approx(1-s\mu/\lambda)^{+}$.

  • X: tables bind before beds. The flow constraint (operating-table-hours) saturates before the stock constraint (holding beds). If beds bind first, the threshold and the governing lever change.
  • Y: above-threshold DOW is waiting-dominated. Once overloaded, the marginal death is a reneging death (ran out of survival window in the queue).
  • Z: work-conserving triage; finite-mean service and survival. No table sits idle while a casualty waits, and $1/\mu$ and $\theta$ have finite means. This makes the overload limit clean and distribution-free to first order.

The modeling choices, each varied in a sensitivity check rather than fit to the data:

  • Queue discipline: the baseline engine serves first-in-first-out within the waiting bay; severity-priority table precedence and air-slot triage are implemented in a committed precedence engine (triage_test.js), and the precedence result (modest, rule-dependent, with naive "sickest-first" air routing backfiring) is reported in Results.
  • Arrival process: nonstationary / batch to match the observed pulses, with plain-Poisson as a robustness comparison.
  • Service model: $M/M/c+M$ (Erlang-A) for the closed-form threshold; $M/G/c+G$ (Whitt fluid) for the distribution-general check.
  • One representative node, stationary within a battle pulse; the network is future work.

Constraints: the hard bounds that fence the levers

  • Wound biology: the survival window $\theta(\text{severity})$ is physiology (hemorrhage timelines, the golden hour). We race it.
  • Minimum service time $1/\mu$: damage-control surgery takes what it takes (Hall: ~94–206 min by ISS)Hall'23. Compressible only so far.
  • Surgical-team inventory: the total pool of teams caps the capacity-allocation lever.
  • Lift / transport: forward casualty evacuation under contested air, and strategic airlift out of theater, caps evacuation throughput and so caps how short the evacuation policy can be.
  • Three endurance limits, kept distinct: (a) the patient-holding window, the FRSD/Role 2 holds a casualty up to 72 h before onward evacuation (ATP 4-02.5); (b) the equipment-set endurance, ≈30 cases / 72 h before the detachment must rest and resupply (ATP 4-02.25)ATP 4-02.25; (c) the Role 1 / casualty-collection-point hold that prolonged field care imposes forward when evacuation fails. The first two bound sustained $\mu$; the third is the forward-axis lever (see Results).

Limitations: what the study cannot settle

The DOW magnitude is unvalidated. No one publishes contested-rear clinical DOW rates, and Ukraine's outcome denominators are closed. The DOW magnitude is therefore a derived output with no real-world figure to check it against. The threshold $\lambda^{*}=s\mu$ is capacity against arrivals; it does not depend on the unobserved survival rate at all. That rate sets only the steepness of the curve above the threshold, so the saturation point holds whatever its true value. Because the magnitude cannot be validated directly, we lead with the checkable inequality $\lambda_{\text{obs}}>s\mu$ and treat the DOW curve as its consequence, then constrain that curve two ways. The two real cases bracket it: Gaza (short hold, copes, CFR 6.7%) and Ukraine (long hold, prehospital death dominates). A sweep then runs the unobserved rate over a bounded range fixed by the TML+ curves, the FM 4-02 evacuation-policy days, and the observed Ukraine hours-to-days.
The open parameter: $f_{\text{surg}}$. The fraction of WIA needing forward surgery lives in the CUI estimator tools (MCET / MPTk), outside open doctrine. Converging open anchors pin it: ≈$0.10$–$0.12$ as a fraction of all WIA (the FST "10–15% of WIA need surgery" planning factor; Soroka's measured ~12% of arrivals), rising to ≈$0.20$–$0.34$ as the operative fraction of casualties who actually present to the node (Staudt, n≈11,000). Which value applies depends on the denominator, and the daily-average load moves with it, but the saturation finding rides on the within-pulse burst, so it survives across the bracket.

Why the threshold is not tautological. $\lambda^{*}=s\mu$ moves only if a doctrinal number or an observed number changes; no analyst preference sits on that path. The DOW curve above the threshold is a theorem of the overload limit. Two prior studies lacked this property: Apache, whose driving parameter was a free analyst knob, and the blockade question, where the measurable proxy never pinned the estimand.
